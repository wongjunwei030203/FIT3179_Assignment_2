Impact of Education on Socio-Economic Outcomes in Malaysia

Exploring Geographic Trends in Secondary School Enrolment

How do secondary school enrolment rates vary across Malaysian states?

This map displays secondary school enrolment rates by state in Malaysia in 2016, with values ranging from around 80% to over 100%. The darker green regions, like Negeri Sembilan and Perlis, show enrolment rates above 100%, indicating strong participation in secondary education. In contrast, lighter green areas, particularly in Sabah, Kelantan, and Putrajaya, reflect lower enrolment rates, around 80-85%, suggesting disparities in educational access across different states

Trends in Education Expenditure, Socio-Economic Factors, and Educational Infrastructure Across States

From 1992 to 2021, education expenditure shows a consistent upward trend, while poverty rates decline. Unemployment remains relatively stable, hovering between 2% and 4%. These categories all spike in 2019, due to the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to widespread economic disruptions and job losses, contributing to fluctuations in both poverty and unemployment.

From 2001 to 2021, there is an overall increase in the number of secondary schools per 1,000 people across every state, reflecting efforts to expand educational infrastructure in line with population growth. Terengganu leads in this number by 2021, potentially indicating higher investment in education or smaller population growth compared to other states. The general upward trend highlights the increasing availability of secondary education across states.

Interrelations of Education, Income, and Crime

Exploring the connections between secondary school enrolment, unemployment, and crime across states, highlighting the socio-economic dynamics at play

There seems to be a positive correlation between school enrolment and household income across states. States that exhibit lower secondary school enrolment rates tend to also report lower mean household incomes, while states with higher enrolment rates generally show higher mean household incomes, highlighting the potential impact of education on economic well-being.

States like Melaka, with its higher mean income, exhibits a lower crime index, suggesting that economic stability may reduce crime rates. Conversely, states with lower household incomes tend to have higher crime indices, indicating a link between economic disadvantage and crime. Outliers include Selangor and Kuala Lumpur, which are primary businesses hubs and display higher crime rates despite high incomes. This highlights the complexities of urbanization and economic disparity.
